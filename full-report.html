AI Agent Adoption

How operators conceptualize agents, where trust lives, and what determines readiness to adopt.

How Operators Conceptualize Agents

The most revealing moment came from P02, who — without any prompting — articulated exactly what makes an agent different from a chatbot: "you're asking it to do something for you instead of just asking a question." Once an operator understands the agent can act, their scrutiny of whether it should act increases substantially.

P01
Agent as smarter replacement

Linear problem-solver

Does pricing on a notepad. The agent slots directly into that workflow and does it better. Mental model: I have a problem → the agent solves it → I act. High trust, low conceptual friction.

P02
Agent as Q&A upgrade

Additive action-taker

Already uses Toast IQ daily for queries, so the shift to action-taking is additive, not disruptive. Has a clear before/after comparison frame — his validation is the most credible in the study.

P03
Agent as co-pilot

Proposes, never acts

Mental model shaped by Uber Eats auto-writing menu descriptions without approval. Didn't reject the agent concept — rejected the assumption that agents should have any autonomy. His ideal agent proposes, never acts.

P04
Agent as daily briefing

Bundled morning intelligence

Unprompted "morning news brief" framing: doesn't want an agent that's always on or always interrupting — wants intelligence bundled into a single daily check-in that fits around how he already starts his day.

Trust Architecture

Trust operates on two distinct layers. Operators extend trust incrementally — institutional trust gets you in the door, data trust is what converts a skeptical observer into a repeat user.

✓ Largely present

Institutional Trust

P01 would approve a bold recommendation "in a rush" because she trusts Toast. P02 uses Toast IQ daily. P04 is a self-described advocate. P03 is the outlier — prior platform trauma has made him skeptical of any system that acts without permission, regardless of brand.

✗ The real gap

Data Trust

Even high-trust participants hesitated when numbers felt opaque. P03 asked "where does this data come from?" multiple times. P01 flagged stale recipe data. P04 wanted the source labeled (integrated system vs. market estimate). P03 needed the $847 projection anchored to unit sales he could verify himself.

→
Institutional trust gets the agent in the room. Data transparency — verifiable sources, anchored projections, labeled estimates — is what earns the second and third use.
The Control Spectrum

All four participants rejected auto-apply, but for meaningfully different reasons. This is not a single preference — it's four different reasons converging on the same behavior.

Participant Position Underlying reason
P01 Would still check with staff, but bar is low Social/operational habit, not distrust of the agent
P02 Verify-and-match before acting Self-described "trust but verify" approach to all AI outputs
P03 Human-in-the-loop required, always Prior trauma — Uber Eats autonomy violation created baseline distrust
P04 Owner sign-off required, no exceptions Organizational hierarchy — pricing is an owner decision, not a manager decision
Key Insight

Even as trust grows, the review step will remain. Operators don't want agents to remove them from the loop — they want agents to reduce the cognitive load before they make the final call. The agent's job is to bring them to a confident decision faster, not to make the decision for them.

Adoption Accelerators & Blockers
What accelerates adoption
  • Transparent, verifiable data sources — brief plain-language explanation of where each number comes from
  • Anchored projections — tie dollar estimates to a unit sales assumption the operator can sanity-check
  • Proof of value before first credit spend — ROI examples from comparable restaurants, video walkthrough
  • Gradual UI introduction — less tech-savvy GMs are a real adoption risk at the staff level
  • Bundled or free-first monetization — credits gate at the highest-intent moment is the single most consistent blocker
What blocks or erodes adoption
  • Opaque data provenance — operators disengage when they can't form a mental model of how a number was generated
  • Auto-apply, even as an opt-in — the framing "you could turn this on" may signal the platform doesn't understand restaurant decision-making
  • Urgency framing misaligned with pricing cadence — QSR vs. FSR vs. farm-to-table operate on very different pricing cycles
  • Unaddressed workflow gaps — an agent that solves 80% of the workflow and leaves 20% manual may be a net-negative if the 20% is harder after the agent has touched the rest

Design Direction

A phased plan for transitioning operators toward the agentic vision — grounded in what the research actually said.

None of these operators rejected the concept. They rejected the execution gaps. The transition to an agentic product is not a single launch — it's a trust-building arc across three phases. Each phase has a clear unlock condition: you don't move to Phase 2 until Phase 1's blockers are resolved. This isn't arbitrary sequencing — it's what the data demands.

Phase 1 — Now

Remove the Structural Blockers

Three issues will suppress adoption regardless of how good the recommendations are. Fix these before anything else. The patterns below are established and low-friction — no new interaction paradigms, just better execution of what's already in the prototype.

1A — Transparent Credits: Show Value Before the Gate

All 4 participants

The credits gate fires at exactly the wrong moment — when an operator has just understood what the agent can do and is ready to act. The current design asks them to spend before they've seen the value, with no dollar translation for what a credit costs. P04's response is the clearest signal: a power user who would rather do manual research than pay.

The fix is not removing the credits model — it's repositioning what gets shown when. Before the confirm gate, surface: cost in dollars (not abstract "credits"), projected revenue recovery from this specific action, and a before/after comparison. Operators are willing to pay for value they can verify. They're not willing to pay for a black box.

Do
  • Show "This will cost $0.40 (2 credits)" in plain dollars
  • Display projected revenue recovery tied to unit sales: "Based on 120 monthly covers, estimated +$680/mo"
  • Offer a free first-use or bundled model — earn the first action, then charge
  • Show ROI examples from comparable restaurants before first spend
Don't
  • Show the credit cost in abstract units without dollar translation
  • Surface the paywall after the operator has committed to the recommendation
  • Show a single projected impact number ($847) with no derivation
  • Frame credits as a scarcity mechanism — that signals distrust
Output Verification Loop — surface confidence before commitment Milestone Escrow — release value, then charge Agent-Assisted Scaffolding — show the work before the ask

1B — Persistent Draft/Review State: No Auto-Apply, Ever

3 of 4 participants

Three participants rejected auto-apply for three different reasons — hierarchy (P04), deliberateness (P03), prior trauma (P03 again, via Uber Eats), and organizational process (P02). The pattern is the same regardless of reason: pricing is a high-stakes, owner-level decision. An agent that publishes without a human sign-off will be turned off, not trusted more.

The agent needs a persistent "Pending Review" state that survives a browser close, a shift change, and a handoff between an operations manager and an owner. The review UI should make the proposed change fully inspectable — what changes, on which items, what the projected impact is, and who approved it.

Do
  • Default all recommendations to "Draft" — require explicit owner publish action
  • Persist the draft across sessions, devices, and role handoffs
  • Show a clear "Awaiting owner approval" state visible to all authorized users
  • Allow the approver to be a different person than the one who ran the agent
  • Send a notification to the designated approver when a draft is ready
Don't
  • Offer auto-apply as an opt-in — even "optional" signals misunderstanding
  • Let the draft expire without surfacing it to the owner
  • Conflate "I ran the agent" with "I approved the change"
  • Make the review state feel like a friction step — it's a feature, not a compromise
Human-in-the-Loop Approval — hard gate before publish Plan-Then-Execute — agent proposes, human approves, then it runs Working Memory via TodoWrite — draft state as legible agent state

1C — Multi-Location Publish Scope: First-Class Control at the Confirm Step

P02 · P04 (4-location operators)

For P02, this is not a nice-to-have — it's a hard dependency. Without Menu Masters integration, the workflow must be repeated per store, making it net-negative. P04 wants a location selector at the approve step. Both operate 4 locations with shared menus. An agent that creates more work than it saves will be abandoned immediately.

The publish scope selector should be a first-class control in the confirm UI: single location, all locations, or select specific locations. For chains using Menu Masters or equivalent sync tools, the integration must propagate atomically — not as a separate step the operator has to remember to do.

Do
  • Show a location scope selector in the confirm UI: "Apply to: [This location] [All 4 locations] [Select…]"
  • Detect Menu Masters / sync tool connections and default to synchronized publish
  • Show a preview of which locations will be affected before confirm
  • Support concept-aware scoping — different concepts may have different pricing strategies
Don't
  • Default to single-location publish without surfacing multi-location options
  • Require a separate Menu Masters sync step after the agent publish
  • Apply the same recommendation uniformly to concepts at different price tiers
Background-to-Foreground Handoff — agent does the propagation, human approves scope Spectrum of Control — let operators choose blast radius

Phase 2 — Next

Build Data Trust & Close Workflow Gaps

Phase 1 removes the reasons operators won't use the agent. Phase 2 builds the reasons they keep coming back. The patterns here address the gap between institutional trust in Toast (high) and data trust in the agent's specific numbers (fragile). This is where the agent stops being a curiosity and becomes a workflow dependency.

2A — Data Provenance Labels: Every Number Needs a Source

P03 (repeated) · P04 · P01

P03 asked "where does this data come from?" multiple times across different recommendation types. Not out of hostility — he couldn't build a mental model of how the numbers were generated, so he couldn't trust them. P04 wanted to know whether cost data came from an integrated system (ExtraChef, Restaurant365) or a market estimate — he'd act on either, but needed to know which. P01 flagged that her recipe data in Toast may be stale from dish evolution.

Every data point that drives a recommendation needs a plain-language provenance label inline — not in a tooltip or a help article. "Based on your invoices in ExtraChef (last synced 3 days ago)" or "Based on aggregate Toast restaurant data in your ZIP code (n=47)". Operators will trust a number they can interrogate far more than a confident number they cannot.

Do
  • Show a brief inline source label on every data-driven number: cost spike source, benchmark source, projected impact basis
  • Flag when source data is stale: "Recipe data last updated 6 months ago — verify before acting"
  • Distinguish integrated data (their own invoices) from aggregate market data (Toast network)
  • Make the sample size visible for benchmarks: "Compared to 47 similar restaurants in your area"
Don't
  • Surface a confident number without any provenance — even a small label changes how operators receive it
  • Bury data sources in a help center article or hover tooltip that requires discovery
  • Treat all data sources as equivalent — operator's own data is higher trust than market averages
Verbose Reasoning Transparency — expose the reasoning behind the number Output Verification Loop — evidence alongside the claim Chain-of-Thought Monitoring — translated into plain-language status, not raw logs

2B — Anchored Projections: Tie Every Dollar to a Verifiable Unit

P03 — "$847 monthly recovery" confusion

P03's reaction to the $847 monthly recovery projection was not "that's too high" or "that's too low" — it was "I can't tell if this applies to one item or everything, and I can't check it." He proposed the fix himself: "if you sold 100 items this month, this is your increase." That's a falsifiable claim operators can verify against their own intuition and sales data.

Every projected impact number should be displayed as a formula the operator can audit: the base assumption (unit volume), the change (price delta), and the resulting impact. If the base assumption is wrong, they can correct it. This shifts the agent from "trust me" to "check my math" — and operators who check the math and find it correct become repeat users.

Do
  • Show: "120 covers/mo × $1.50 price increase = +$180/mo on this item"
  • Let operators adjust the unit volume assumption inline — "Based on your actual sales: 94 covers" with an edit affordance
  • Aggregate projections clearly: "Across all 3 affected items: +$520/mo estimated"
  • Show a range when data quality is uncertain: "+$400–$680/mo depending on demand elasticity"
Don't
  • Show a single dollar figure without the formula that produced it
  • Mix per-item and total-menu projections in the same display without clear labeling
  • Present projections with false precision — "exactly $847" is less trustworthy than "+$780–$900"
Output Verification Loop — confidence score needs the evidence, not just the number Abstracted Code Review — show intent-level summary, always offer drill-down

2C — Modifier Cascade Transparency: Surface What Else Changes

P04 — live revenue incident

P04 described a real incident: a base price change created modifier arbitrage where customers ordered a cheap dog patty to avoid paying for a double burger. He's now doing this analysis manually before every price change. The prototype treats pricing as item-level — it is not. A price change on one item can silently break pricing logic across modifiers, add-ons, substitutions, and combo meals.

Every pricing recommendation should include a "downstream impact" section that surfaces all affected modifiers, substitutions, and add-ons before the operator approves. This isn't a warning — it's the difference between the agent being helpful and the agent creating new problems. Operators who catch a modifier issue in the review step become evangelists.

Do
  • Show all modifiers, add-ons, and substitutions linked to any changed base item
  • Flag items where the modifier pricing may create arbitrage after the base change
  • Allow the operator to cascade the price change to modifiers in the same confirm flow
  • Highlight the revenue risk of not updating cascaded items: "Customers could order X at the old effective price via Y modifier"
Don't
  • Treat a base price change as an isolated item-level action
  • Require operators to manually check modifier impact after the agent has already "approved" the change
  • Bury modifier warnings below the fold — they are as important as the primary recommendation
Human-in-the-Loop Approval — cascade review as part of the approval gate Plan-Then-Execute — plan includes full cascade impact before execution Background-to-Foreground Handoff — agent surfaces the cascade, operator decides

2D — Gradual UI Transition: Earn Each Interface Upgrade

P04 — GM adoption risk · P01 — video onboarding request

P04 flagged that a dramatic UI shift risks non-adoption among less tech-savvy GMs — the people who will actually use the tool day to day. P01 wanted email notifications linking to YouTube tutorials showing the feature working before committing credits. The agentic interface is a new paradigm; operators shouldn't be thrown into the deep end on first contact.

The entry point should be the existing menus page — contextual suggestions surfaced inline, familiar to operators who already manage menus there. The agent interface emerges from that context, not as a separate tool they have to navigate to. Each interaction that goes well earns the right to show more of the agent's capability. Think of it as a spectrum, not a switch.

Do
  • Start with contextual suggestions on the menus page — familiar entry point, no new navigation required
  • Introduce the full agent chat interface only after at least one successful recommendation interaction
  • Send email onboarding with video walkthroughs before operators encounter the agent in-product
  • Show a "see an example" preview of a completed recommendation before asking for the first credit spend
  • Offer a sandbox/preview mode for operators to explore without live changes
Don't
  • Launch the full agent interface as the first touchpoint
  • Assume all GMs have the same comfort with AI tools as the owner who evaluated the prototype
  • Use in-product tooltips as the primary onboarding mechanism — they're skipped
Spectrum of Control — tab suggestion → scoped action → full agent (Cursor model) Agent-Assisted Scaffolding — AI scaffolds, operator refines, no blank-page problem Progressive Autonomy with Model Evolution — start constrained, expand as trust grows

2E — Post-Publish Coordination: Approving a Change Is Not the Last Step

P01 · P04 — downstream action needs

P01 wants an automated internal staff note generated on publish. P04 wants deep-link redirects to team announcements, website updates, and social media tools. P03 accepted a static checklist without friction. The delivery mechanism varies but the underlying need is consistent: after a price change goes live, a cascade of downstream tasks must happen — and operators currently carry that list in their heads.

At minimum, the post-publish step should surface a smart checklist. For operators who want more, one-click deep-links into Toast's announcement and marketing tools turn the checklist from a reminder into an accelerator. This is a low-cost, high-trust-building addition that operators will notice and remember.

Do
  • Show a contextual checklist immediately after publish: "Staff notified? Website updated? POS auto-updated ✓"
  • Auto-check items that the system knows are handled (e.g., POS sync)
  • Offer deep-links for manual items: "→ Post a staff announcement" "→ Update your website menu"
  • For printed menus, surface a "reprint reminder" — P01 and P03 both use physical menus
Don't
  • Show "Change published successfully" and close the flow — that's where the real work starts
  • Automate downstream actions without operator confirmation — reminders are enough
  • Show the same generic checklist to every operator — a QSR operator and a fine dining FSR have different downstream steps
Background-to-Foreground Handoff — agent completes publish, hands off downstream checklist Working Memory via TodoWrite — post-publish checklist as shared task surface Proactive Trigger Vocabulary — auto-surface the checklist on publish event, no prompt needed

Phase 3 — Later

Expand Agency & Unlock New Value

These are the moves that turn the agent from a pricing assistant into a margin-protection platform. They require Phase 1 and 2 to be solid — operators who don't yet trust the data won't act on supplier recommendations, and operators who had a bad modifier cascade experience won't adopt competitive benchmarking. Sequence matters.

3A — Supplier Alternative Recommendations: Protect Margins, Don't Just Raise Prices

P01 — "magic wand" request

P01's request is the most differentiated product idea in the entire study. When an ingredient cost spikes, the current prototype has one response: raise your price. P01's preferred response: find me a cheaper supplier who sells the same ingredient at the original price. This reframes the agent's entire cost-spike use case from reactive (raise prices) to proactive (protect margins).

This is a different and potentially stickier value proposition — one that no current Toast competitor addresses. The design implication is an expanded cost-spike recommendation card: primary action remains "adjust price" but a secondary action surfaces "alternative suppliers" with a comparison table (supplier, current price, lead time, minimum order). Operators choose their lever; the agent provides both options.

Do
  • When a cost spike is detected, surface two options: raise price OR find a supplier alternative
  • Show supplier alternatives with enough detail to act: name, price per unit, lead time, Toast partnership status
  • Let operators save preferred suppliers and get alerted when a connected supplier can undercut their current one
  • Frame this as margin protection — "protect your margin without touching your menu price"
Don't
  • Lead with price increase as the only option for cost spikes
  • Show supplier alternatives without integration into the actual ordering workflow — a list is not enough
  • Launch this before Phase 2 data provenance is in place — operators won't trust supplier data from an opaque source
Latent Demand Discovery — P01's workaround (manual supplier research) becomes a first-class feature Democratization of Tooling — operators without supplier relationships get the same intelligence as large chains Episodic Memory Retrieval — remember preferred suppliers across sessions

3B — Competitive Regional Benchmarking: Market Intelligence as a First-Class Signal

P02 · P03 — independently surfaced

Both P02 and P03 independently described doing competitive benchmarking manually today — P03 researches what nearby restaurants charge for comparable dishes, P02 tracks regional specials and promotions. Neither was prompted. Neither framed it as a nice-to-have; both framed it as a regular operational task they'd be relieved to automate. This is untapped demand, not a hypothetical feature.

The design implication is a benchmarking module that goes beyond internal cost analysis into market positioning. Key nuance from P03: geographic benchmarking alone breaks for multi-concept operators — a casual pasta at $16 and an upscale pasta at $25 are not the same product competing in the same market. The agent must be concept/tier-aware when generating benchmark comparisons.

Do
  • Surface competitive pricing benchmarks as a distinct recommendation type — not just a background input
  • Show data source and sample size: "Based on 47 comparable restaurants within 2 miles"
  • Apply concept-tier awareness: allow operators to specify their price positioning (value / mid-range / upscale) to get relevant comps
  • Include regional specials and promotional patterns, not just static menu prices (P02's specific ask)
Don't
  • Mix upscale and casual pricing benchmarks without concept-tier segmentation
  • Launch benchmarking before data provenance (Phase 2) is resolved — "where does this data come from?" will be the first question
  • Frame benchmarking purely as "you're underpriced" — also surface "you're well-positioned" to build confidence
Latent Demand Discovery — both P02 and P03 already built manual versions of this Output Verification Loop — benchmark claims need evidence, not just assertions Team-Shared Config as Code — concept-tier config shared across locations

3C — The Daily Brief: Bundle Intelligence Into an Existing Habit

P04 — "morning news brief" mental model

P04's unprompted framing is the most nuanced interaction design insight in the study: operators don't want an agent that's always on or always interrupting. They want intelligence bundled into a single daily check-in that fits around how they already start their day. The Toast Now app morning pop-up — forecasts, recommendations, and alerts in one dismissible card — is the right delivery surface, not a persistent notification stream.

This is the Phase 3 culmination of the trust arc: by this point, operators have seen accurate recommendations, verified the data, experienced the review flow, and trust the agent enough to make it part of their morning routine. The daily brief is not a new product — it's the reward for earning operator trust through Phases 1 and 2.

Do
  • Bundle recommendations, cost alerts, and benchmarks into a single morning card — one thing to check, not three
  • Make it dismissible — operators who aren't ready to act today should be able to clear it without consequence
  • Persist unaddressed recommendations as a draft until the operator acts — don't re-surface the same alert every day
  • Let operators configure what goes in their brief: some want cost alerts, some want benchmarks, some want both
Don't
  • Launch a persistent notification stream before operators have established trust with individual recommendations
  • Make the brief non-dismissible or tie it to a credit spend — that's a dark pattern that will destroy trust
  • Use urgency framing ("act today!") for operators on quarterly pricing cycles — P02's reaction was clear
Proactive Trigger Vocabulary — morning brief as auto-trigger on daily session start Episodic Memory Retrieval — brief is personalized based on operator's history and preferences Context Window Auto-Compaction — brief surfaces only what's new, not a full history replay

Core Design Principles

Six principles that apply across all three phases — derived from the research and grounded in established agentic UX frameworks.

Principle 1

Propose, Never Act

The agent's job is to bring operators to a confident decision faster — not to make the decision for them. Draft/review is not a UX compromise; it's the product. Every action the agent could take autonomously should instead be surfaced as a proposal with a clear human approval step.

Grounded in: Human-in-the-Loop Approval, Plan-Then-Execute, all 3 auto-apply rejections
Principle 2

Show the Math

Every number the agent surfaces needs a traceable source and a verifiable formula. Operators don't need to understand how the model works — they need to be able to check the inputs. An anchored, falsifiable claim builds more trust than a confident unverifiable one.

Grounded in: Output Verification Loop, Verbose Reasoning Transparency, P03's projection anchoring request
Principle 3

Earn Each Step of Autonomy

Operators are not waiting for the concept to improve — they're waiting for the execution to catch up. Trust expands incrementally: institutional trust gets you in the door, data trust converts a skeptical observer into a repeat user, and workflow completeness turns a repeat user into an advocate.

Grounded in: Spectrum of Control, Progressive Autonomy, Adoption Readiness Gradient
Principle 4

Close the Full Loop

An agent that solves 80% of the workflow and leaves 20% as manual is not a 0.8× improvement — it may be a net negative if the 20% is harder after the agent has touched the rest. Every workflow the agent enters must exit cleanly, including modifier cascades, multi-location sync, and post-publish downstream tasks.

Grounded in: Background-to-Foreground Handoff, P02's Menu Masters dependency, P04's modifier cascade incident
Principle 5

Fail Loudly, Recover Gracefully

How an agent fails communicates more about its reliability than how it succeeds. Every failure path — stale data, missing cost information, blocked modifier access — needs as much design attention as the happy path. "The agent encountered an error" is never an acceptable message.

Grounded in: Agent Circuit Breaker, Denial Tracking, Hook-Based Safety Guard Rails
Principle 6

Fit the Existing Habit, Don't Create a New One

The menus page is where operators already go. The morning check-in is when they already review their day. The agent should surface intelligence inside those existing touchpoints, not require operators to adopt a new tool, learn a new navigation, or change their routine to get value.

Grounded in: P04's daily brief model, P01's contextual menus page request, Agent-Friendly Workflow Design
