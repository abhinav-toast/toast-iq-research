JTBD
Jobs To Be Done
What operators are actually trying to do
A feature request tells you what someone asked for. A JTBD tells you why they'd hire a product in the first place. Every job below is inferred from a real moment in the research — not from what participants said they wanted.

Prioritization TL;DR
Start here — preconditions
JTBDs 2.1, 2.2, and 2.3 must be solved first. If operators can't trust the numbers, none of the other jobs can be completed — regardless of how well the rest of the product works.
Highest differentiation
JTBD 1.1 is the only job that reframes what the product is — from a pricing tool to a margin protection tool. Every other JTBD improves the current product; this one expands its identity.
Structurally different
JTBD 3.1 can't be solved with better UI. It requires a different state model — persistent drafts with role-based routing. It's about fitting into an organizational process the product currently ignores.
The design constraint
Operators will hire this agent as a research assistant and work-product preparer, never as a decision-maker. Every design decision that pushes toward autonomous action works against the grain.
The underlying pattern: operators aren't trying to delegate decisions to an agent — they're trying to make better decisions faster. The JTBD is never "have the AI decide for me." It's always: give me the right information, at the right moment, in a form I can verify and act on within my organizational process.
Cluster 1
Margin Protection Protect the business when costs change
1.1
The Reactive Pricing Job
When costs spike, I need all the paths — not just "raise the price"
›
"When ingredient costs spike and I need to protect my margins, I want to understand all the paths available to me — not just 'raise your price' — so I can choose the response that does the least damage to my guests and my business."
◆ Highest differentiation opportunity
Pain behind it
P01 is doing pricing math on a notepad. The agent as currently designed gives her one answer (raise the price). But her actual job is margin protection — raising prices is just one of several ways to do that. The pain isn't that she lacks a recommendation; it's that she lacks options.
What a hired solution looks like
An agent that responds to a cost spike with a set of paths — raise price / find alternative supplier / reduce portion / bundle differently — with projected margin impact per path. The operator chooses the response; the agent quantifies the tradeoff.
1.2
The Silent Risk Job
Show me everything a price change touches before I publish it
›
"When I change a price on any menu item, I want to see every other part of my menu that will be affected before I publish, so I can avoid creating pricing errors that cost me revenue."
Pain behind it
P04 had a live incident — a base price change created modifier arbitrage that customers exploited. His current process is now entirely manual: before any price change, he traces modifier dependencies by hand. The agent treats pricing as item-level when it's actually graph-level.
What a hired solution looks like
An agent that automatically maps the modifier graph before surfacing any pricing recommendation, and surfaces arbitrage risks before the confirm step — not after publish.
Cluster 2
Confident Decision-Making Be able to verify what the agent tells you
2.1
The Verification Job
Show me the assumption so I can check the math myself
›
"When I see an AI-generated projection, I want to understand the assumption it rests on so I can verify the math against what I actually know, so I can decide whether to trust it or reject it."
▲ Precondition — solve before anything else
Pain behind it
P03 saw "$847 monthly recovery" and immediately became skeptical — not because the number was necessarily wrong, but because he had no way to check it. He's been in the restaurant business since 1990. The pain is that the agent gave him a conclusion without the inputs, which made it feel fabricated rather than credible.
What a hired solution looks like
Every projection tied to a verifiable unit assumption: "Based on 847 units sold in March, a $1 increase recovers $847/month." He can open his POS, check March's units, and confirm or dispute the basis. The agent passes the falsifiability test.
2.2
The Data Integrity Job
Tell me if the data you're using actually reflects my operation today
›
"When I'm about to act on an AI recommendation, I want to know whether the data the agent is working from actually reflects my operation today, so I don't make decisions based on stale or wrong inputs."
▲ Precondition — solve before anything else
Pain behind it
P01's recipes evolved after she originally entered them in Toast. The cost data the agent uses is months out of date. She has high trust in Toast as a platform, but she can't fully trust recommendations because she doesn't trust the inputs — which puts verification work back on her.
What a hired solution looks like
An agent that runs its own data health check before surfacing recommendations, and leads with a data quality signal ("your chicken sandwich recipe was last updated 8 months ago") rather than surfacing a recommendation built on potentially stale data.
2.3
The Provenance Job
Where does your market data come from, and are those restaurants actually like mine?
›
"When an agent tells me I'm underpriced compared to my local market, I want to understand where that market data comes from and how comparable those restaurants actually are to mine, so I can decide whether the benchmark is meaningful for my operation."
▲ Precondition — solve before anything else
Pain behind it
P03 operates two concepts at different price tiers in the same market. A geographic benchmark that averages across all nearby restaurants will systematically tell his upscale concept it's "overpriced." Without knowing the data source and composition, he can't tell whether the recommendation is brilliant or irrelevant.
What a hired solution looks like
Benchmarks surfaced with explicit composition ("compared to 28 full-service restaurants within 5 miles, average check $35–55") so he can evaluate whether the peer group is actually comparable. Multi-concept operators need concept-tier-aware benchmarking, not geographic averaging.
Cluster 3
Organizational Alignment Fit into the real approval process
3.1
The Stakeholder Alignment Job
Help me get everyone aligned before I publish — the agent currently ignores this step entirely
›
"When a pricing decision needs to be made, I want to present a fully-formed case to the decision-makers in my organization so we can decide together, so I can publish with confidence that everyone who needs to be aligned is aligned."
■ Can't be solved with better UI — requires a different state model
Pain behind it
P04's pricing decisions require sitting down with the owners. P02 needs organizational sign-off. P03, even as the owner himself, requires deliberate personal review before acting. The current design assumes the interacting user and the approving user are the same person — they almost never are.
What a hired solution looks like
A persistent draft state that captures the full recommendation package and routes it to the right approver. The GM runs the agent, the agent produces a draft, the owner reviews and approves. The draft doesn't expire overnight — it waits until the organizational process completes.
3.2
The Post-Change Coordination Job
After I publish, I shouldn't have to carry the follow-up checklist in my head
›
"When I publish a price change, I want every system and every person that needs to know to be updated, so I'm not managing a cascade of manual follow-up tasks from memory."
Pain behind it
P01 and P04 both described carrying the post-publish checklist in their heads: reprint menus, update the website, tell the staff, post on social. The pain isn't that it's hard — it's that it's invisible. The agent celebrates the publish moment without acknowledging that the operator's work isn't done.
What a hired solution looks like
A post-publish coordination package built into the draft — not as an afterthought, but as a required step in the approval workflow. Staff notification drafted. Website update prompted. Social post suggested. Each item either deep-linked to the relevant tool or acknowledged as manual.
Cluster 4
Operational Execution Run the decision at scale, on the right cadence
4.1
The Multi-Location Consistency Job
Change it once, have it propagate correctly everywhere
›
"When I make a pricing decision that applies to all my locations, I want to make the change once and have it propagate correctly everywhere, so I'm not doing the same work four times or risking inconsistency between locations."
Pain behind it
P02's most direct statement: "we don't want to have to go through it four times." Without Menu Masters integration, the agent requires per-location execution. That's not a minor inconvenience — it makes the agent net-negative for multi-location operators with shared menus.
What a hired solution looks like
Scope-aware execution. The operator declares scope at draft creation (this location / all locations / select). The agent propagates accordingly. For operators using Menu Masters or equivalent sync tools, the publish step triggers the integration — not manual repetition.
4.2
The Cadence-Aligned Action Job
"Effective tomorrow" framing doesn't match how my business actually works
›
"When a recommendation is ready, I want to act on it at the right moment in my operational cycle, not in response to urgency framing that doesn't match how my business actually works."
Pain behind it
P02 changes prices quarterly because customer perception of frequent changes is negative. The prototype's "effective tomorrow" framing felt not just irrelevant but slightly alarming — as if the agent didn't understand how his operation works. An agent that misreads cadence feels miscalibrated, which makes operators question what else it's getting wrong.
What a hired solution looks like
Recommendation timing derived from the operator's actual cadence, not from a universal urgency template. A high-sensitivity issue for a quarterly-cadence operator gets framed as "consider for your next menu review" not "act now." The agent that knows when not to push earns more trust than one that's always urgent.
Cluster 5
Situational Awareness Know what's happening without digging for it
5.1
The Daily Orientation Job
Give me the picture in under 60 seconds — I shouldn't have to go find it
›
"When I start my day, I want to quickly understand what needs my attention across my business, so I can prioritize and get to work without digging through multiple tools."
Pain behind it
P04's "morning news brief" framing was entirely unprompted. He articulated a specific mental model: one card, forecasts + recommendations + alerts, dismissable. Situational awareness currently requires active navigation across multiple reports. The operator has to go find the picture; the picture doesn't come to them.
What a hired solution looks like
A single daily briefing card that bundles what's changed, what needs review, and what the agent recommends — structured to be readable in under 60 seconds and dismissable if nothing urgent is present.
5.2
The Competitive Awareness Job
Stop making me manually scout what comparable restaurants near me are charging
›
"When I'm planning promotions or reviewing my pricing, I want to know what comparable restaurants near me are doing, so I can position my offer without having to manually scout the competition."
Pain behind it
P02 and P03 both do this manually today. P03 physically researches what nearby restaurants charge for comparable dishes. Neither framed it as a product gap — they described it as a regular operational task. The pain is time spent on a low-value information-gathering activity that an agent with market data access could trivially automate.
What a hired solution looks like
Competitive context surfaced alongside menu items and pricing recommendations — not as a separate dashboard, but as ambient intelligence woven into the existing workflow.
The Underlying Pattern
The design constraint that runs through everything
Across all five clusters, the same structural tension surfaces: operators are not trying to delegate decisions to an agent — they're trying to make better decisions faster. The JTBD is never "have the AI decide for me." It's always some form of: give me the right information, at the right moment, in a form I can verify and act on within my organizational process.
The implication for design: the agent is hired as a research assistant and a work-product preparer, not as a decision-maker. Every JTBD above is satisfiable within that constraint — and none of them require autonomous action to deliver real value.
What this means for the agent
Every feature that pushes toward autonomous action is working against the grain. propose → draft → execute decomposition isn't a UX pattern — it's a direct response to how operators actually want to hire this product.
What this means for prioritization
Solve the trust infrastructure first (Cluster 2). Without it, operators won't let the agent reach the point where Clusters 3, 4, and 5 even matter. The preconditions gate everything else.
