Cross-Interview Findings

Patterns that appeared across multiple participants — the strongest signal in the data.

Critical · 4/4

Credits / Monetization — Universal Friction

Every participant flagged unprompted. Reactions ranged from conditional acceptance to hard veto.
P01P02P03P04
›
Finding
Every participant noticed the credits model unprompted. Reactions ranged from conditional acceptance (P01, P02, P03) to hard veto (P04). The common thread: operators already feel Toast is expensive, and a pay-to-use gate at the moment of highest intent is the wrong place for a paywall. P04's signal is the most telling — they'd abandon the workflow and do the research manually rather than spend credits. No one asked for a feature change; they asked for transparency (what does a credit cost in dollars?) and proof of ROI before first spend.
Design implication
Show per-credit dollar value + projected revenue recovery before the confirm gate. Consider a free first-use or bundled-usage model.
High · 3/4

Owner Sign-Off Gate — Hard Requirement

P02, P03, and P04 all rejected auto-apply. Framing varied; the pattern did not.
P02P03P04
›
Finding
P02 cited organizational process, P03 cited deliberateness and control after a bad Uber Eats experience with auto-publishing descriptions without permission, P04 cited owner hierarchy. The pattern is the same regardless of framing: pricing changes are high-stakes decisions that cannot be unilaterally triggered by an AI.
Design implication
The agent must support a persistent draft/review state that survives until an owner or designated approver acts on it. Auto-apply should not exist, even as an opt-in, until significant trust is established.
Recurring · 2/4

Post-Publish Staff Communication — Recurring Need

P01 wants auto staff notification. P04 wants deep-link redirects. Underlying need is consistent across both.
P01P04
›
Finding
P01 wants an automated internal staff note generated on publish. P04 wants deep-link redirects to team announcements, website updates, and social media tools. P03 accepted a static checklist without friction. The delivery mechanism varies but the underlying need is consistent: approving a price change is not the last step — operators need to coordinate downstream communication.
Design implication
Post-publish "things to consider" should offer at minimum a static checklist, and ideally one-click deep-links into Toast's announcement and marketing tools for operators who want them.
Deal-breaker · 2/4

Multi-Location Publish Scope — Deal-Breaker for Multi-Location Operators

P04 wants a location selector. P02 requires Menu Masters integration. Without either, the workflow is net-negative.
P02P04
›
Finding
P04 wants a location selector at the approve step. P02 requires Menu Masters integration — without it, the workflow must be repeated per store, making it net-negative. Both operate 4 locations with shared menus.
Design implication
Publish scope (single location vs. all vs. select) must be a first-class control at the confirm step, with integrations for tools like Menu Masters for chains running identical menus.
Independent · 2/4

Competitive Benchmarking — Independently Surfaced

P02 asked for regional pricing context as a promotional input. P03 does this manually today.
P02P03
›
Finding
P02 asked for regional competitive pricing context as a promotional strategy input. P03 described manually doing this today and framed automation as a genuine upgrade. Neither was prompted. Neither framed it as a nice-to-have.
Design implication
Competitive benchmarking is a distinct value proposition beyond internal cost analysis — worth exploring as a separate signal or module.

Operator-Specific Standout Findings

The most differentiated insight from each participant — not covered elsewhere.

P01

"Protect my margins, don't just raise my prices"

The magic wand request: find a cheaper supplier instead of recommending a price hike.
›
When ingredient costs spike, the prototype has one response: raise your price. P01's preferred response: find me a cheaper supplier at the original price. This reframes the agent's cost-spike response from "raise your price" to "here is another way to protect your margin" — a meaningfully different and potentially stickier value proposition that the current prototype does not address.
I would do the thing where I can reference product lists from all the suppliers around me, and instead of raising the price of this dish, it would give me somewhere else to buy the cheese that we're losing money on here.
P01 — Owner/Operator, FSR

P03

Data provenance is a prerequisite for trust

Asked "where does this data come from?" multiple times. Unanchored projections erode trust.
›
Asked "where does this data come from?" multiple times for both cost spike and benchmark data. Also raised that unanchored projections (e.g., "$847 monthly recovery") erode trust — needs projections tied to a unit sales assumption so the math can be independently verified. Operates two concepts at different price tiers and flagged that geographic benchmarking alone will generate wrong recommendations for multi-concept operators.
Even just one number — if you sold a hundred items this month, this is what your increase would be.

P04

Modifier cascades are a real, hidden risk

Shared a live incident where a base price change created modifier arbitrage that cost the business revenue.
›
Shared a live incident where a base price change created modifier arbitrage that cost the business revenue. The agent must surface which modifiers, substitutions, and add-ons are affected by any pricing change — not just the base item. Also surfaced the clearest monetization warning: a power user who would abandon the tool rather than buy more credits.
You think it's a simple thing — everything goes up a dollar — but then you get into modifiers and add-ons. How far does this go is what I would want to know.
