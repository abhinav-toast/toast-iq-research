Emerging Themes
Pricing Cadence Mismatch
New
›
P02 stated that their restaurant changes prices quarterly or semi-annually at most, because customers notice and react negatively to frequent price changes. The prototype's "effective tomorrow" framing landed as misaligned with how they actually operate.
Why it matters
If the agent's urgency framing ("act now," "effective tomorrow") is calibrated for farm-to-table or high-volatility cost environments, it will feel irrelevant or even alarming to QSR operators with stable menus — potentially eroding trust in the recommendation engine overall.
"We're not gonna change our prices weekly or even monthly. It's more of, like, a quarterly maybe, maybe half a year kind of thing, because people will notice that you're changing prices up and down and they don't like that."
Competitive Benchmarking as a Distinct Want
New
›
P02 unprompted asked for regional competitive context — what other restaurants in their state or area are doing for specials and pricing. This was entirely unprompted and distinct from the cost-based recommendations already in the prototype.
Why it matters
This is a distinct signal beyond the pricing optimization use case — operators want peer benchmarking as a trust anchor and a discovery tool for promotional strategy, not just cost-based pricing guidance. The agent's value proposition extends into market intelligence.
"What other stores are doing as well, what other restaurants across, like, your same state or region, kind of, like, what they're doing for their specials."
Multi-Location Publish Scope
Recurring · 2/4
›
P02 flagged that because all four locations share the same menu via Menu Masters, any price change must apply across all locations simultaneously. Approving a change per-store is not viable.
Why it matters
Recurring theme — seen in P02 and P04. The publish/approve step is currently designed as a single-location action. Multi-location operators need a scope selector or integration with menu sync tools before this workflow is usable.
"To make this work, it would have to, like, immediately be part of menu masters for us to want to do this, because we don't want to have to go through it four times."
Credits / Monetization Friction
Recurring · 4/4
›
P02 paused on the credits gate and asked how credits are acquired, what they cost, and whether there are non-monetary ways to earn them. Would need ROI examples before spending credits the first time.
Why it matters
P04 had harder resistance (would not pay beyond free allotment); P02 is softer but still needs a clear value-exchange story and proof before committing. The credits model creates friction at the moment of highest intent — just before a recommendation is acted on.
"I would probably need examples before pressing it the first time to know if it was going to be worth it or not. Because you want to weigh out, because if something else also costs credits, you want to weigh out what you want to learn more about, what's more valuable."
