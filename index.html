Must Fix Before Shipping

Structural blockers that will suppress adoption regardless of recommendation quality.

Critical · 4/4

Credits model will suppress usage

Universal signal. Operators already feel Toast is expensive. P04 would investigate manually rather than pay.
P01P02P03P04
›
Full finding
Every participant noticed the credits model unprompted. Reactions ranged from conditional acceptance (P01, P02, P03) to hard veto (P04). The common thread: operators already feel Toast is expensive, and a pay-to-use gate at the moment of highest intent is the wrong place for a paywall.
Design implication
Show per-credit dollar value + projected revenue recovery before the confirm gate. Consider a free first-use or bundled-usage model. P01 wants a video walkthrough before first spend.
Do
  • Show "$0.40 (2 credits)" — dollar translation, not abstract units
  • Show projected ROI before asking for payment
  • Offer free first use or bundled credits
  • Show ROI examples from comparable restaurants
Don't
  • Show cost in abstract credit units without dollar value
  • Surface the paywall after operator has committed to a recommendation
  • Show a single projected impact number with no derivation
Key quote — P04
Toast makes a lot of money just off of us already with a regular account. If it came down to wanting to know this thing, I would just go investigate it myself.

Auto-apply cannot exist

Three different reasons, same behavior. The agent needs a persistent draft/review state — not a publish button.
P02P03P04

Full finding

  • Conflate "I ran the agent" with "I approved the change"

I almost felt like they hijacked my descriptions. As long as I have control of it and don't get hijacked — that's a big issue.

The goal of each phase is not to add features — it's to earn the right to more autonomy. Operators are ready to hand the agent more control as soon as they trust what it already does.
Phase 1 — Now

Remove the Structural Blockers

Fix these before anything else. These patterns are established and low-friction — no new interaction paradigms, just better execution of what's already in the prototype.

1A

Transparent Credits — Show Value Before the Gate

Reposition what gets shown when. Operators will pay for value they can verify. They won't pay for a black box.
›
What to build
Before the confirm gate, surface: cost in dollars (not abstract "credits"), projected revenue recovery from this specific action tied to unit sales, and a before/after comparison. Operators are willing to pay for value they can verify.
Do
  • Show "This will cost $0.40 (2 credits)" — dollar translation
  • "Based on 120 monthly covers, estimated +$680/mo"
  • Offer free first-use or bundled model
  • Show ROI examples from comparable restaurants before first spend
Don't
  • Show credit cost in abstract units without dollar translation
  • Surface the paywall after operator has committed to the recommendation
  • Show a single projected impact number with no derivation

1B

Persistent Draft/Review State — No Auto-Apply, Ever

The draft/review state is not a UX compromise — it's the product. Every action the agent could take autonomously should instead be a proposal with a human approval step.
›
What to build
A persistent "Pending Review" state that survives a browser close, a shift change, and a handoff between an operations manager and an owner. The review UI must make the proposed change fully inspectable — what changes, on which items, projected impact, who approved it.
Do
  • Default all recommendations to "Draft" — require explicit owner publish action
  • Persist across sessions, devices, and role handoffs
  • Allow the approver to be different from who ran the agent
  • Notify the designated approver when a draft is ready
Don't
  • Offer auto-apply even as an opt-in
  • Let the draft expire without surfacing it to the owner
  • Conflate "I ran the agent" with "I approved the change"

Phase 2 — Next

Build Data Trust & Close Workflow Gaps

Phase 1 removes the reasons operators won't use the agent. Phase 2 builds the reasons they keep coming back.

2A

Data Provenance Labels

Every number needs a traceable source. P03 asked "where does this data come from?" multiple times.
›
What to build
Every data point that drives a recommendation needs a plain-language provenance label inline — not in a tooltip or help article. "Based on your invoices in ExtraChef (last synced 3 days ago)" or "Based on 47 similar restaurants in your ZIP code." Operators who can interrogate a number will trust it far more than one that arrives confidently with no source.
Do
  • Inline source label on every data-driven number
  • Flag stale data: "Recipe data last updated 6 months ago"
  • Show sample size for benchmarks: "n=47 restaurants"
Don't
  • Surface a confident number with no provenance
  • Bury sources in help center articles
  • Treat all data sources as equivalent trust level
Agentic patterns
Verbose Reasoning TransparencyOutput Verification Loop
2B

Anchored Projections

P03 couldn't verify the $847 figure. He proposed the fix himself: "if you sold 100 items, this is your increase."
›
What to build
Show every projected impact number as a formula operators can audit: base assumption (unit volume), the change (price delta), the resulting impact. Let operators adjust the unit volume assumption inline. This shifts the agent from "trust me" to "check my math" — operators who check and find it correct become repeat users.
Do
  • "120 covers/mo × $1.50 increase = +$180/mo on this item"
  • Let operators edit the unit volume assumption inline
  • Show a range when uncertain: "+$400–$680/mo"
Don't
  • Show a single dollar figure without the formula
  • Mix per-item and total-menu projections without clear labeling
  • Present projections with false precision
Key quote — P03
Even just one number — if you sold a hundred items this month, this is what your increase would be.
2C

Modifier Cascade Transparency

Surface what else changes. P04 had a live revenue incident from a base price change that broke modifier pricing.
›
What to build
Every pricing recommendation must include a "downstream impact" section surfacing all affected modifiers, add-ons, and substitutions. Flag items where modifier pricing may create arbitrage after the base change. Allow the operator to cascade the change to modifiers in the same confirm flow.
Do
  • Show all linked modifiers, add-ons, substitutions before approve
  • Flag potential arbitrage: "Customers could order X at old price via Y modifier"
  • Let operators cascade the price change to modifiers in the same flow
Don't
  • Treat a base price change as an isolated item-level action
  • Bury modifier warnings below the fold

Phase 3 — Later

Expand Agency & Unlock New Value

These moves turn the agent from a pricing assistant into a margin-protection platform. They require Phase 1 and 2 to be solid first.

3A

Supplier Alternative Recommendations

Protect margins, don't just raise prices. A different and potentially stickier value prop.
›
What to build
When a cost spike is detected, surface two options: raise price OR find a supplier alternative. Show alternatives with enough detail to act: name, price per unit, lead time, Toast partnership status. Operators choose their lever; the agent provides both options.
Do
  • Two options on cost-spike card: raise price OR alternative supplier
  • Comparison table: supplier, price/unit, lead time, status
  • Let operators save preferred suppliers for future alerts
Don't
  • Lead with price increase as the only option for cost spikes
  • Show supplier list without integration into actual ordering workflow
  • Launch before Phase 2 data provenance is in place
Agentic patterns
Latent Demand DiscoveryEpisodic Memory RetrievalDemocratization of Tooling
3B

Competitive Regional Benchmarking

Both P02 and P03 independently described doing this manually today. Proven demand.
›
What to build
A benchmarking module that goes beyond internal cost analysis into market positioning. Concept/tier-aware — let operators specify their price positioning (value / mid-range / upscale) to get relevant comps. Include regional specials and promotional patterns, not just static menu prices.
Do
  • Concept-tier-aware comparisons: casual vs. upscale
  • Show data source + sample size: "n=47 restaurants within 2 miles"
  • Include specials and promotions, not just prices
Don't
  • Mix upscale and casual benchmarks without tier segmentation
  • Launch before data provenance (2A) is resolved
  • Frame only as "you're underpriced" — also surface "well-positioned"
3C

The Daily Brief

Bundle intelligence into an existing habit. P04's "morning news brief" mental model is the Phase 3 payoff.
›
What to build
Intelligence bundled into a single daily check-in that fits around how operators already start their day. The Toast Now app morning pop-up — forecasts, recommendations, and alerts in one dismissible card — is the right delivery surface, not a persistent notification stream. This is the Phase 3 culmination of the trust arc.
Do
  • Bundle recommendations, cost alerts, benchmarks into one morning card
  • Make it dismissible — operators not ready today can clear it
  • Persist unaddressed recommendations as a draft, don't re-surface daily
  • Let operators configure what goes in their brief
Don't
  • Launch a persistent notification stream before trust is established
  • Use urgency framing for operators on quarterly pricing cycles
  • Make it non-dismissible or tie it to a credit spend

Core Design Principles

Six principles derived from the research — applicable across all three phases.

Principle 1

Propose, Never Act

Draft/review is not a UX compromise — it's the product. Every action the agent could take autonomously should be surfaced as a proposal with a human approval step.

Human-in-the-Loop Approval · Plan-Then-Execute · all 3 auto-apply rejections
Principle 2

Show the Math

Every number needs a traceable source and a verifiable formula. An anchored, falsifiable claim builds more trust than a confident unverifiable one.

Output Verification Loop · Verbose Reasoning · P03's projection anchoring request
Principle 3

Earn Each Step of Autonomy

Trust expands incrementally. Institutional trust gets you in the door. Data trust converts a skeptic into a repeat user. Workflow completeness turns a repeat user into an advocate.

Spectrum of Control · Progressive Autonomy · Adoption Readiness Gradient
Principle 4

Close the Full Loop

An agent that solves 80% of the workflow and leaves 20% manual may be a net-negative if that 20% is harder after the agent has touched the rest.

Background-to-Foreground Handoff · P02's Menu Masters dependency · P04's modifier cascade
Principle 5

Fail Loudly, Recover Gracefully

How an agent fails communicates more about its reliability than how it succeeds. Every failure path needs as much design attention as the happy path.

Agent Circuit Breaker · Denial Tracking · Hook-Based Safety Guard Rails
Principle 6

Fit the Existing Habit

The menus page is where operators already go. The morning check-in is when they already review their day. Surface intelligence inside those touchpoints — don't require a new routine.

P04's daily brief model · P01's contextual menus page request · Agent-Friendly Workflow Design
