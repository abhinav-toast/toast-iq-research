Emerging Themes
Credits / Monetization Friction
Recurring · 4/4
›
P04 expressed immediate, unprompted resistance to the credits model, framing it as an unnecessary add-on given their existing Toast bill. They would not purchase additional credits once a free allotment was exhausted — they would simply investigate manually instead.
Why it matters
This is a direct signal that the credits unlock mechanism may suppress usage among high-value, fully-integrated Toast customers who already feel the platform is expensive. The most telling signal: they would abandon the workflow and investigate manually rather than pay — meaning the model could suppress usage among exactly the customers who would use it most.
"I just think it, toast, it makes a lot of money just off of us already with a regular account. I don't know if I would, you know, if I went beyond my 50 credits, I definitely wouldn't purchase it. If it came down to wanting to know this thing, I would just go, okay, I'll just investigate it myself."
Modifier/Add-On Cascade as a Hidden Pricing Risk
New
›
P04 described a real-world incident where a base price change exposed unintended modifier arbitrage — customers were ordering a cheap dog patty as a workaround to avoid paying for a double burger. They want the agent to explicitly surface which modifiers, add-ons, and substitutions are affected by a pricing recommendation.
Why it matters
The prototype currently treats price changes as item-level actions. P04's real incident demonstrates that modifier cascades are a material revenue and operational risk that the agent should surface proactively — "is it just that dish or is it anything that correlates with it?"
"I think that's the one thing I know when doing price changes, that you think it's a simple thing, okay, everything goes up a dollar, but then you get into different things like modifiers and add-ons and substitutions. That affects it and, you know, it's a little, it goes a little bit more deeper than that. So how far does this go is what I would be wanting to know."
Owner Sign-Off as a Gate on Any Pricing Change
Recurring · 2/4
›
P04 stated unambiguously that pricing or menu changes always require sitting down with the owners before publishing — auto-apply is categorically off the table.
Why it matters
For multi-location, owner-operated restaurants, the approval workflow needs to accommodate a stakeholder who is not always the person interacting with Toast IQ. The agent must support draft/review states that persist until an owner can weigh in — the current prototype doesn't have this concept.
"anything, anything, pricing or menu changes are always. Myself and the owners sitting down and discussing. And then making a decision and then, yeah, publish, publishing it from there or whatever."
Notification Channel — "Daily News Brief" Mental Model
New
›
P04 prefers email for recommendations but also articulated a specific mental model for the Toast Now app: a morning pop-up "news brief" that bundles forecasts, recommendations, and notifications in one dismissible card.
Why it matters
Operators want AI recommendations bundled into an existing daily check-in habit rather than surfaced as a separate, always-on notification stream. This is a pattern worth testing in the notification design — bundle into the morning routine, not a standalone alert.
"I think having your daily forecasts or your daily overview or, news brief, if you will, that would be, you know, kind of an all-in, here's your news brief for the day, you know, click here for what were your forecasts, click here for a couple of recommendations, and then if you're not interested, you can just close it."
Downstream Action Deep-Links
New
›
After approving a change, P04 wanted the "things to consider" section to include clickable redirects — one for pushing a team announcement to staff, one for updating the website, and one for creating a social media post.
Why it matters
Operators don't just need to approve a change — they need to coordinate the downstream communication. Deep-links into existing Toast tools (announcements, marketing) could meaningfully reduce the post-publish workload and make the approve flow feel complete rather than a half-finished action.
"I just, I think, like, a redirect on that would be good for any of that, you know. Even, social media. You know. Create a social media post. Or whatever, where you can say, hey, not that you want to advertise price increases, but I think those kind of things are helpful when you have those, those ease of use."
