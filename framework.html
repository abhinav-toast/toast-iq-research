The Non-Negotiable Rule
Phase ordering cannot be skipped. The temptation is to jump to Phase 3 because that's where the product demo lives — but without Phase 1 earning data trust, operators arrive at the draft screen already skeptical of the numbers inside it.
The fastest path to full autonomy is paradoxically the most conservative one. Build skills that demonstrate the agent is right before building skills that let it act.
Where Most Products Stall
Moving from "agent prepares, human approves" to "agent acts, human reviews summary" requires trust capital that can't be manufactured through UX alone — it has to be accumulated through repeated, accurate, unremarkable Phase 3 behavior.
Design implication: Phase 3 should be explicitly built to generate that trust signal — outcome tracking, accuracy scores, and closed-loop feedback. Without it, operators have no evidence base to expand into Phase 4.

The Five Phases
Click any phase to expand
1
Phase 1 · Intelligence Layer
Sensing & Surfacing
Agent role: Observer No actions. Only signals. Earns the right to be noticed.
›
This phase is about the agent earning the right to be noticed. Operators need to experience the agent being right before they trust it to act. The interaction pattern is purely informational — the agent surfaces signals without asking anything of the operator.
Foundational skills to build now
Data Audit
Before any recommendation, checks whether underlying data is current and flags gaps
›
Before surfacing any recommendation, the agent silently checks whether the underlying data (recipes, costs, menu structure) is current and complete. Gaps are flagged with a "data health card" — the recommendation is withheld until inputs are trustworthy.
Why it builds trust
P01's blocker: stale recipe data meant she couldn't trust the numbers. An audit skill makes inputs trustworthy before outputs are shown — decoupling data trust from recommendation trust.
Anomaly Detection
Monitors cost spikes, sales drops, margin compression in the background — surfaces contextually in the menu editor
›
Monitors cost spikes, sales drops, and margin compression continuously. Surfaces them contextually — in the menu editor, not in a separate tool — so operators encounter insights where work is already happening.
Why it builds trust
Operators learn the agent is watching things they aren't. Each accurate alert is a trust deposit. This builds ambient trust before any recommendation is ever shown.
Data Provenance Labeling
Every number shown includes a plain-language source label — "Based on regional Toast restaurant averages"
›
Every number shown to the operator includes a plain-language explanation of its source: "Based on your invoicing data via ExtraChef" or "Based on regional Toast restaurant averages (340 comparable restaurants)."
Why it builds trust
P03's core ask — asked "where are you getting your data from?" multiple times. Without provenance labels, operators disengage before acting. Labels make claims falsifiable and verifiable.
Competitive Benchmarking Signal
Surfaces local market pricing context alongside menu items — not a recommendation, just a signal
›
Surfaces local market pricing context alongside menu items as a passive signal — not a call to action. "Nearby restaurants in your category are charging $18–22 for a comparable dish. You're at $16.49."
Why it builds trust
P02 and P03 both do this manually today. Automating it earns immediate credibility — the agent is doing something they already value but find time-consuming.
Interaction pattern
Passive contextual cards. Appear where work is already happening (menu editor, product mix report). Dismissable. No CTA beyond "learn more." No credits consumed at this stage.
2
Phase 2 · Recommendation Layer
Propose & Explain
Agent role: Advisor Proposes, never acts. Full reasoning visible. Operator still owns execution.
›
Once the agent has demonstrated it sees things accurately (Phase 1), it earns the right to make recommendations. The pattern shifts from surfacing signals to presenting a complete case: here's what I see, here's why it matters, here's what I suggest, here's what I expect will happen.
Skills to build
Anchored Projection
Ties every dollar estimate to a verifiable unit sales assumption — "Based on 847 units sold last month..."
›
Every dollar projection is tied to a verifiable unit sales assumption. Shows the math explicitly: "Based on your 847 units sold last month, a $1.50 price increase recovers ~$1,270/month." Operators can sanity-check against their own knowledge.
Why it builds trust
P03's core ask: "Even just one number, if you sold 100 items this month, this is what your increase would be." Unanchored projections ($847/month with no unit basis) erode trust rather than build it — big unverifiable numbers feel fabricated.
Confidence Scoring
Shows certainty level per recommendation with brief reasoning — "High confidence, based on 6 months of your sales data"
›
Each recommendation shows a confidence level with a brief explanation of why: "High confidence — based on 6 months of your own sales data + 340 comparable Toast restaurants." Operators apply their own judgment on top of a confidence signal rather than accepting or rejecting blindly.
Why it builds trust
P02's explicit request. Habitual verify-and-match operators need a confidence signal they can reason about — not just a number, but a brief explanation of what's behind it.
Modifier Cascade Preview
When a base item price changes, surfaces every affected modifier, add-on, and substitution with downstream pricing impact
›
When a base item price change is proposed, the agent surfaces every modifier, add-on, and substitution that would be affected and shows the downstream pricing impact — including whether any existing modifiers would become arbitrage opportunities.
Why it builds trust
P04's live incident: a base price change exposed modifier arbitrage (customers ordering a cheap dog patty to avoid paying for a double burger). The agent preventing that incident once earns more trust than any onboarding flow.
Alternatives Generation
For cost-spike recommendations, proposes multiple response paths: raise price, find alternative supplier, reduce portion
›
For cost-spike recommendations, the agent proposes multiple response paths — raise the price, find an alternative supplier at the original cost, or reduce portion size. The operator chooses the response that fits their context.
Why it builds trust
P01's "magic wand" — wants the agent to find a cheaper supplier instead of always recommending a price increase. Reframes the agent from "price optimizer" to "margin protector" — a meaningfully different and stickier value proposition.
Interaction pattern
Recommendation cards with progressive disclosure. Default view: headline recommendation + expected outcome. Expanded view: full reasoning, data sources, confidence, alternatives. Operator reads, decides, acts manually. No draft yet — operator still owns execution.
3
Phase 3 · Draft Layer
Prepare & Preview
Agent role: Preparer Builds the complete change package. Human reviews and approves.
›
The agent transitions from advisor to co-worker. It doesn't just recommend — it prepares everything needed to execute: the price change, affected modifiers, the downstream checklist, and the staff notification draft. The operator's job becomes review-and-approve, not build-and-execute.
Skills to build
Draft State Management
Creates a persistent, named draft that survives sessions and can be shared with owners for sign-off
›
Creates a persistent, named draft that survives browser sessions and can be shared with other owners for sign-off before publishing. The draft is a first-class object — not a transient state.
Why it builds trust
P04, P02, and P03 all require owner approval before any pricing change goes live. Draft persistence makes the agent compatible with how multi-stakeholder restaurants actually make decisions — the approver isn't always the person who interacted with the agent.
Scope Selector
At draft creation, operator sets scope: this location, all locations, or select locations
›
At draft creation, the operator sets scope: this location, all locations, or a selection of locations. The draft is scoped accordingly and Menu Masters / cross-location sync tools are invoked appropriately.
Why it builds trust
P04 and P02's hard requirement — both operate 4 locations with shared menus. Without scope selection, the workflow forces them to repeat the approval process per store, making it net-negative.
Downstream Package Builder
Automatically assembles the post-publish checklist — staff notification, website update prompt, social template
›
Automatically assembles the post-publish coordination package as part of the draft: staff notification draft, website update prompt, social post template, and a list of affected menu sections that need reprinting. Approval triggers publish + downstream coordination in one step.
Why it builds trust
P01 and P04's downstream coordination need. P01 wants an automatic staff note; P04 wants deep-link redirects to staff announcements and social tools. The package builder addresses both patterns without requiring the operator to remember what to do after approving.
Sandbox Preview
Shows exactly what the menu will look like after the draft is applied — before committing
›
Shows the operator exactly what the menu will look like after the draft is applied — modified items, affected modifiers, updated pricing — before any live change is committed. The sandbox is a read-only preview, not a staging environment.
Why it builds trust
P01's explicit request. Eliminates the fear of "what will this look like live." Operators with printed menus (P03) especially need to see the final state before approving, since a wrong approval has physical consequences.
Interaction pattern
Draft workspace. Operator reviews a complete change package — not a list of recommendations to manually implement, but a ready-to-publish bundle. Approve, reject, or modify individual items. Approval triggers publish + downstream coordination in one step.
4
Phase 4 · Supervised Autonomy
Execute & Report
Agent role: Executor with guardrails Acts within operator-defined rules. Reports every action. Future phase.
›
The agent now executes, but only within operator-defined rules. The operator sets the guardrails; the agent operates within them. Every action is logged with a full audit trail and is reversible within a defined window.
Skills to build (future)
Guardrail Definition
Operator sets rules: "Never increase any item by more than 15%." Agent operates within them without prompting.
›
Operator sets explicit rules: "Never increase any item by more than 15%." "Never change prices at more than 2 locations simultaneously." "Always require my approval for items over $25." The agent operates within these without prompting unless a boundary is approached.
Why it builds trust
Operators feel in control of the envelope even when the agent is acting inside it. The guardrail definition interface is itself a trust-building interaction — it forces operators to articulate their own constraints.
Audit Trail
Every agent action logged with timestamp, reasoning, data source, and expected outcome
›
Every agent action is logged with timestamp, reasoning, data source, and expected outcome. The operator can review the log at any time. The log is the paper receipt of agent actions — it makes the agent's behavior legible and contestable.
Why it builds trust
P03's core concern: "I need to know what's happening." An audit trail is the paper receipt of agent actions — it makes autonomous behavior legible and gives operators something to point to when something goes wrong.
Reversibility Window
Any agent-executed change can be rolled back within 24 hours with one click
›
Any agent-executed change can be rolled back within 24 hours with one click, with a full explanation of what will be restored. The rollback is itself logged and does not require approval.
Why it builds trust
Removes the fear of irreversible mistakes. Operators take more risks when they know there's an undo. The presence of a rollback option is not a failure state — it's a trust signal that accelerates adoption.
Cadence Awareness
Agent learns the operator's actual pricing cadence and stops surfacing "act now" urgency to quarterly-change operators
›
The agent learns from operator behavior and aligns recommendation timing to their actual pricing cadence — quarterly for QSR, monthly for FSR, event-driven for farm-to-table. Stops surfacing "act now" urgency to operators who batch changes.
Why it builds trust
P02's pricing cadence mismatch — "we change prices quarterly at most." An agent that learns when not to push builds more trust than one that's always urgent. Irrelevant urgency erodes the overall signal-to-noise ratio.
Interaction pattern
Exception-based. Agent acts silently within guardrails and surfaces a daily summary of what it did and why. Operator reviews the summary, not each individual action. Intervention required only when an action approaches a guardrail boundary.
This phase requires trust capital accumulated through repeated, accurate, unremarkable Phase 3 behavior. It cannot be designed or UX'd into existence — it must be earned.
5
Phase 5 · Full Autonomy
Monitor & Self-Correct
Agent role: Domain operator Owns a domain end-to-end. Reports outcomes, not actions. Far-future phase.
›
The agent now owns a domain — margin protection, competitive pricing, menu performance — and manages it end-to-end. The operator sets the objective; the agent handles the execution loop including monitoring outcomes and adjusting. This phase is only reachable after the preceding phases have accumulated sufficient trust capital.
What unlocks this phase
Operator has seen the agent be right enough times to trust its judgment (Phase 1–2). Operator has reviewed and approved enough drafts to trust its execution (Phase 3). Operator has watched the agent act within guardrails without incident long enough to expand the envelope (Phase 4). All four of these prerequisites must be present — none can be skipped.
No participant in this study is anywhere near Phase 5 readiness. Design work targeting this phase is premature until Phase 3 is stable and trusted across a meaningful operator cohort.
Always-On Skills
Present from Phase 1 through Phase 5
Three embedded skills that must be present from day one because they underpin every phase. They don't belong to any single phase — removing them at any point makes the next phase harder to adopt.
💬
Explainability
Every recommendation, projection, and action surfaces its reasoning in plain language — not just a score
Not just a confidence score — an actual sentence: "I'm recommending this because your pork belly cost increased 23% in the last 60 days while comparable restaurants in your area have already adjusted prices upward." This skill is what makes data trust possible. Without it, every other skill is harder to adopt. Operators don't trust black boxes — they trust agents that show their work.
⚕️
Data Health
Before any recommendation is shown, silently checks data completeness and freshness — surfaces a health card if inputs are stale
Before any recommendation is shown, the agent silently checks data completeness and freshness. If data is stale or missing, it surfaces a data health card first — not the recommendation. This decouples data trust from recommendation trust. Operators stop doubting the agent's logic when they know the agent itself is checking its own inputs. P01's stale recipe data is the canonical example of why this skill is load-bearing.
🛑
Human Override
At every phase, every agent action has a visible, immediate override — not buried in settings, but in the primary workflow
At every phase, every agent action has a visible and immediate override. Not buried in settings — surfaced in the primary workflow. The presence of an override is not a failure state; it's a trust signal. P03's Uber Eats trauma is the canonical example: the moment she felt she couldn't override descriptions, she disengaged entirely. Operators adopt agents faster when they know the off switch is always one tap away.
Design Principles
01
Contextual surfacing over dedicated surfaces
Recommendations should appear where operators already work, not in a separate "AI" section. The agent comes to the operator; the operator doesn't navigate to the agent.
02
Progressive disclosure — never lead with complexity
Default view is always the headline and recommended action. Reasoning, data, and alternatives are one level deeper. Complexity available on demand, invisible by default.
03
Show the math — every projection is falsifiable
Operators with decades of experience will spot a bad number immediately — and will trust the agent more after they've tested it and found it correct. Unverifiable projections erode trust faster than any UI issue.
04
Respect the pricing cadence
Urgency language is segment-specific. What's "act now" for a farm-to-table is "noted for Q3 review" for a QSR. An agent that learns when not to push builds more trust than one that's always urgent.
05
Build for the owner, design for the GM
The final approver and the daily user are often different people. The skill architecture must support both — the GM interacts with the agent daily; the owner reviews drafts and sets guardrails.
06
Propose, never act unilaterally
Draft/review is non-negotiable until Phase 4 guardrails are in place and trusted. Any UI pattern that shortcuts the human review step — including "auto-apply" — is a trust destroyer regardless of accuracy rate.
The Foundational Bet
Strategic framing
"Operators are not waiting for a better UI. They're waiting for an agent that earns trust through accuracy, transparency, and restraint before it earns the right to act. The fastest path to full autonomy is paradoxically the most conservative one — build skills that demonstrate the agent is right before building skills that let it act. Every phase of trust earned is a phase of friction removed from the next."
